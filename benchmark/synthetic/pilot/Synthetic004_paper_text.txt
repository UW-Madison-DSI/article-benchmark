MATERIALS AND METHODS

Title: Modeling the effect of water activity on dairy microflora growth in cream cheese

Sample Preparation and Experimental Design

Cream cheese samples were prepared at laboratory scale using a standardized protocol that allowed controlled adjustment of the water activity of the matrix while keeping other compositional parameters as constant as possible (Author et al., 2018). Water activity was modified by adjusting the salt-in-moisture content during formulation, yielding a set of experimental matrices spanning a_w values from 0.93 to 0.99. After preparation, samples were portioned into 50 g aliquots, packaged in sterile containers, and stored at the four target temperatures of 4, 8, 12 and 18 °C. Each combination of water activity and storage temperature was prepared in triplicate within each of three independent manufacturing batches.

Microbiological analysis was performed at predetermined sampling times, with a higher sampling frequency during the initial phases of growth. The target organism was a representative member of the dairy microflora typically associated with cream cheese spoilage, inoculated at a level of approximately 3.0 ln cfu/mL to produce a clearly detectable growth response within the experimental time frame. Population density was determined using standard plate-count techniques following the procedure described by Author et al. (2019), with results converted to natural log units for compatibility with the primary growth model.

Modeling Framework

The modeling framework adopted in this work consists of three interrelated equations. A primary model describes the temporal evolution of the microbial population in the cheese matrix, a secondary model describes the dependence of the maximum specific growth rate on temperature, and an alternative primary formulation is used as a basis of comparison when the growth curves do not exhibit a measurable lag phase. The cross-references between these equations are highlighted in the subsections that follow.

Primary Growth Model with Lag Phase

For experimental conditions in which a clear lag phase was observed prior to the onset of exponential growth, the population dynamics were described using the Baranyi growth model with explicit lag:

[1]   N(t) = N_0 + \mu A(t) - \ln\left(1 + \frac{e^{\mu A(t)} - 1}{e^{N_{\max} - N_0}}\right)

where t is time, expressed in hours (h), and N(t) is the population at time t, expressed in natural log units (ln cfu/mL). The parameter N_0 is the initial population of the target organism, set to 3.0 ln cfu/mL on the basis of the nominal inoculation level. The parameter N_{\max} is the maximum population attained by the target organism in the cream cheese matrix, fixed at 20.0 ln cfu/mL based on the asymptote of the experimental curves. The parameter \mu is the maximum specific growth rate, expressed in h^{-1}, and is the principal parameter to be estimated from the data; its dependence on temperature is described separately through Eq. [2].

The adjustment function A(t) introduces the lag phase into the model and is defined as A(t) = t + (1/\mu) · ln(exp(-\mu t) + exp(-h_0) - exp(-\mu t - h_0)). Here h_0 is a dimensionless parameter representing the initial physiological state of the cells at the start of the experiment; it captures the metabolic work that the inoculum must perform to adapt to the cheese matrix before resuming exponential growth. The value of h_0 was estimated globally from the full dataset and held constant across temperature conditions within a given water activity level. Secondary Model for Temperature Dependence

The temperature dependence of the maximum specific growth rate was described using the Ratkowsky square-root model, a robust empirical relationship that has been extensively applied in predictive microbiology (Author et al., 2017). The model is formulated as

[2]   \sqrt{\mu} = b (T - T_{\min})

where T is the temperature, expressed in degrees Celsius (°C). The parameter b is a regression coefficient that quantifies the slope of the relationship between the square root of the growth rate and the temperature, expressed in (°C)^{-1} h^{-0.5}; an indicative value of b in the present work was 0.04 (°C)^{-1} h^{-0.5}, with refined estimates obtained for each water activity level. The parameter T_{\min} is the conceptual minimum temperature for growth of the target organism, expressed in °C, with an indicative value of −1.5 °C used as a starting point for parameter estimation. As described by the original authors of this formulation, T_{\min} should be interpreted as a conceptual (extrapolated) value rather than the observed minimum temperature at which growth was actually detected.

The model defined in Eq. [2] rests on two principal assumptions. First, the relationship between the square root of \mu and the temperature T is linear in the suboptimal temperature range, which corresponds to the conditions of the present work. Second, T_{\min} is a conceptual extrapolated value that need not coincide with the temperature at which growth is empirically undetectable. The parameter b and T_{\min} were estimated by linear regression of \sqrt{\mu} against T, with \mu values obtained from the fitting of Eq. [1] at each storage temperature, as detailed in the next subsection.

Alternative Primary Model Without Lag

For experimental conditions in which the inoculum was pre-adapted to the storage temperature and no detectable lag phase was observed, an alternative primary formulation was used for comparison. The Baranyi growth model without lag was applied in its log10-based formulation:

[3]   N(t) = N_{\max} + \log_{10}\left(\frac{e^{\mu t}}{e^{\mu t} - 1 + 10^{N_{\max} - N_0}}\right)

where t is the elapsed time since the start of growth, expressed in hours (h), and N(t) is the population at time t of the target organism, expressed as the decadic logarithm of the population density (log10 cfu/g). The parameter N_{\max} is the maximum population density attained by the target organism in this alternative formulation, set to 8.0 log10 cfu/g, and N_0 is the initial population density, set to 2.5 log10 cfu/g. The parameter \mu is the specific growth rate, expressed in h^{-1}, where \mu has the same biological meaning as in Eq. [1] although the numerical value is referred to a log10 rather than a natural-logarithm population scale.

This alternative formulation rests on two assumptions: no lag phase is considered, so that growth begins immediately at the specified rate, and the population follows logistic-type approach to the fixed maximum density N_{\max}. Comparison of the fits provided by Eq. [1] and Eq. [3] for individual datasets was used as a diagnostic for the presence or absence of a measurable lag phase under each combination of water activity and temperature.

Parameter Estimation Procedure

Estimation of the parameters of Eqs. [1] and [3] was performed by nonlinear least-squares regression using the Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm. Initial values of \mu were derived from the maximum slope of a moving-window linear regression applied to the central portion of each growth curve. For Eq. [1], h_0 was first estimated globally from the full dataset, and the temperature-specific values of \mu were then obtained with h_0 fixed at its pooled estimate. The Ratkowsky model defined in Eq. [2] was fitted to the \mu values obtained at each storage temperature for a given water activity level using linear regression on the transformed scale \sqrt{\mu} versus T. Parameter values for the fitted secondary model are reported in Table 2.

Goodness-of-fit was assessed using the root mean square error of the residuals and the coefficient of determination, complemented by the bias and accuracy factors recommended in standard predictive microbiology practice. Confidence intervals for the parameters were obtained from the asymptotic standard errors returned by the regression procedure. The combined modeling framework described above provided a coherent and self-consistent description of the kinetics of the dairy microflora in cream cheese as a function of both temperature and water activity.
